Why 2 random vectors are orthogonal in high dimention?

August 23, 2007 on 3:01 pm | In Misc |

In a recent comment, Nhat Vo wrote :

You wrote â€œIndeed, in hight dimensional spaces, 2 random vectors are almost always orthogonalâ€. Do you have any reference or proof for that?

Here is a math proof. Consider 2 random vectors in dimension s. Let’s say that they are on the unit (s-1)-sphere (only directions matter), and that the first one is fixed.

Then we draw a second vector uniformly on the sphere, and we look if he is orthogonal with the fixed one. For that purpose, let’s use the (s-1)-dimensional spheric parametrization. A point on the sphere is parametrized with (s-1) angles : a_1…a_{s-1}. Let’s consider that the first vector is “vertical”, and that the first angle parameter is the angle between the two vectors.
The spherical parametrization says that all angles are in [0,Pi], except one in [0,2Pi]. Moreover the uniform distribution, in this coordinate system becomes (page 67, in french) :

p(a_1…a_{s-1}) \propto \prod_{i=1}^{s-2} (sin a_i)^{s-i-1}

(this generalizes the 3D spherical surface element dS = sin \phi d\theta d\phi)

Then we just have to calculate p(a_1) as a function of s to see if it concentrates around pi/2, meaning that the 2 vectors are more and more orthogonal. This is easy by marginalizing other angles in p() because every angles are independent. So we get :

p(a_1) = \int_{a_i \neq a_1} p(a_1…a_{s-1})da_i \propto (sin a_1)^{s-2}

which gives the following graph. We clearly see the concentration phenomena that Terry was talking about. When s gets bigger, we obtain a narrower distribution, and the probability that a_1 is epsilon-close to Pi/2 becomes larger. Image for s=2, 4, 40, 100 and 1000 :

In the limit, s-> \infty, all vectors are almost surely orthogonal. For instance, the scalar product of 2 random functions over [0,1] is 0 (remember that such functions are almost surely not continuous)
