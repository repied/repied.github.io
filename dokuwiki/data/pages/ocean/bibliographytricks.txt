== How did Francis do the bibliography in his manuscript using !LaTeX and !BibTeX? ==
Disclaimer: This may not be the only way to do what I wanted and you may not want to do that.

What follows is an quick explanation of the packages I used and why in order to have a bibliography that suited my needs.
More precisely, part of the preamble of my document was:
 \documentclass[12pt]{book}
 \usepackage[francais]{babel}

It's important to note my document is in french. It would have been simpler if not.


=== Issues with default configuration ===
I wanted to have a meaningful description of the articles I cited in my text. For me, "[=[66]]" (style plain) or "[LBDM+04]" (style alpha) did not apply.
I wanted something more along the lines of "Lebeltel /et al/ (2004)" and no bibliography style I tried did that (I may not have tried everything).


=== Solution ===
Then I found the [=natbib] package (in the standard distribution). And inserted the following line in my preamble:
 \usepackage{natbib}

And chose the [=abbrvnat] style:
 \bibliographystyle{abbrvnat}

A [=\cite{LBDM04}] command was then expanded into "Lebeltel et al (2004)". And the corresponding entry looked like "O. Lebeltel, P. Bessiere, J. Diard and E. Mazer. Bayesian robot programming. /Advanced Robotics/, 16(1) :49-79, 2004"


=== Complement ===
There are several details that didn't exactly suit my taste. First, the 'and' conjunction between the two last authors and the fact that french syntax was used in the bibliography even if I only cite one work in french.


==== Replacement of 'and' by 'et' ====
The first issue is simply addressed with the [=abbrvnat-fr] bibliography style. It was not in my distribution so I found it there: http://www.lsv.ens-cachan.fr/~markey/BibTeX/bst/abbrvnat-fr.bst (local version: {file:abbrvnat-fr.bst}).

I therefore use:
 \bibliographystyle{abbrvnat-fr}


==== English syntax for references ====
The [=babel] package with [=francais] argument changes spacing according to french syntactic rules.
In the end, I decided to change the language to english just for the bibliography. English language must be loaded:
 \usepackage[english,francais]{babel}
The order is important as the active language is one specified last (but one can switch at the beginning of the document).

To change language we use: [=\selectlanguage{english}].
Changing the language to english for the bibliography also changes its title to 'Bibliography'. So I respecified the title with [=\renewcommand{\bibname}{Bibliographie}].

I have an appendix after my bibliography so I switched back to french: [=\selectlanguage{french}].

In the end, my preamble includes:
 \usepackage[english,francais]{babel}
 \usepackage{natbib}

and I declared my bibliography as follows:
 \selectlanguage{english}
 \renewcommand{\bibname}{Bibliographie}
 \bibliographystyle{abbrvnat-fr}
 \bibliography{...}
 \selectlanguage{french}


==== Cool things with [=natbib] ====

[=natbib] defines some commands to replace [=\cite]. The two main ones I used are [=\citep] and [=\citet].

[=\citet] only puts the year in parentheses to use the reference in the sentence:
 \citet{LDBM04} presentent la programmation bayesienne.
expand into "Lebeltel /et al./ (2004) presentent la programmation bayesienne."

[=\citep] puts the whole citation into parentheses:
 On peut appliquer le bayesien a la programmation des robots \citep{LBDM04}.
becomes "On peut appliquer le bayesien a la programmation des robots (Lebeltel /et al./ 2004)."

You can also add some infos:
 La programmation bayesienne definit un cadre de travail \citep[figure~1]{LBDM04}.
writes "La programmation bayesienne definit un cadre de travail (Lebeltel /et al./ 2004, figure 1)."

The last thing I did was to change the delimitors with [=\bibpunct{(}{)}{~;}{a}{}{,}] in my preamble.

Some more details can be found there: http://merkel.zoneo.net/Latex/natbib.php ([french version http://merkel.zoneo.net/Latex/natbib.php?lang=fr]).
